# ShopRates — Mortgage & Home Loan Article Framework

The reader is trying to buy, refinance, or finance an investment property in Tennessee — and they're stuck on one question. Will they qualify? What rate can they actually get? Can they buy without tax returns, with a 580 score, with 5% down, as a self-employed borrower, as an investor past the conventional 10-property cap? They're comparing lenders in browser tabs at 10pm. They're deciding whether ShopRates is the broker who gets it.

Write to that person. Every section either answers the qualification question they came with, removes a cost surprise before it kills the deal, or moves them toward a rate quote.

This is not the panic-driven legal market. The reader is motivated but not in crisis. Authority here comes from precision on numbers — rates, limits, down payments, fees — and from naming the exact borrower scenario the page is built for.

---

## Audience and voice

Write as a sharp, experienced mortgage broker would talk to a serious borrower — someone who shops 60+ lenders and knows the products cold. The voice combines:

- A loan originator who has actually structured FHA, VA, USDA, conventional, jumbo, and non-QM files
- A DSCR/investor-loan specialist who understands LTV tiers, ratio thresholds, and portfolio scaling
- A self-employed-lending expert who knows bank statement, P&L, 1099, and asset-depletion programs
- A local Middle Tennessee market guide — Nashville, Franklin, Brentwood, Murfreesboro, the suburbs

Avoid: thin SEO filler, generic AI hedging ("it's important to note that..."), keyword stuffing, vague rate talk ("rates are low right now!"), and anything that reads like a content mill wrote it.

**Example — voice calibration:**

*Weak (generic, no expertise signal):*
> A DSCR loan is a great option for real estate investors. It lets you qualify based on the property instead of your income. There are many benefits to DSCR loans, and they can help you grow your portfolio.

*Strong (specific, operator-level):*
> A DSCR loan qualifies on the property's cash flow, not your W-2 — so the tax write-offs that sink you on a conventional file don't matter here. The number that drives everything is the ratio: rent divided by PITIA. Hit 1.25 with a 700+ score and you're looking at 20% down and the best pricing. Drop below 1.0 and most lenders cap you at 75% LTV, so plan on 25% down. And unlike conventional, there's no 10-property ceiling — which is the whole reason investors past their fourth or fifth door switch over.

The strong version names the actual qualifying mechanism (rent ÷ PITIA), the ratio tiers, the LTV consequence, and the strategic reason a specific borrower chooses it.

## Grounding facts (current as of mid-2026 — verify before publishing)

Use these as defaults. Mortgage numbers move; confirm anything rate-sensitive before a page goes live, and frame rates as ranges with a "verify today" nudge rather than hard quotes.

**Rates (mid-2026 range):** 30-year fixed conventional running in the mid-6% range (~6.4–6.6%); 15-year fixed in the high-5s (~5.7–5.9%); FHA and VA typically a touch below conventional; jumbo near or slightly above conventional; DSCR roughly 0.5–1.5% above conventional; non-QM bank statement/P&L loans roughly 0.5–1.5%+ above conventional. Always present rates as "as of [date], subject to change" — never as a guarantee.

**2026 conforming loan limit:** Baseline $832,750 (one-unit). Nashville metro is a high-balance market at **$1,029,250**. High-cost ceiling nationally is $1,249,125. Above the county limit = jumbo.

**FHA:** 3.5% down at 580+ FICO; 10% down at 500–579. DTI commonly up to 43%, higher with compensating factors. Lifetime MIP (1.75% upfront + ~0.55% annual) unless 10%+ down (then cancels after 11 years). 2026 FHA floor $541,287; ceiling $1,249,125 — confirm the specific TN county limit.

**VA:** $0 down with full entitlement, no maximum price (lender overlays still apply). No monthly mortgage insurance. Funding fee ~2.15% first use (rolled into loan), reduced/waived for some borrowers; IRRRL funding fee 0.5%. Approval driven by DTI + residual income.

**USDA:** $0 down, owner-occupied, property must be in an eligible (rural/some suburban) area. Income capped at 115% of area median — TN ~$119,850 for a 1–4 person household (confirm county). 1% upfront guarantee fee + 0.35% annual fee.

**Conventional:** As little as 3% down (first-time) / 5% typical; PMI until ~20% equity, then removable (unlike FHA MIP). 620+ score typical.

**DSCR:** Qualifies on rent ÷ PITIA. Minimum ratio commonly 1.0–1.25 (some programs allow below 1.0 or "no-ratio" at lower LTV). 620+ score floor, 700+ for best terms/leverage. 20–25% down typical; below-1.0 ratio usually caps at 75% LTV. 3–6 months reserves common. No portfolio cap — scales past the conventional 10-property limit.

**Non-QM / self-employed:** Bank statement loans use 12–24 months of deposits (expense factor often ~50%); 620–660+ score; 10–25% down. P&L loans use a CPA-prepared profit-and-loss (net profit as income), often 640+. 1099-only and asset-depletion programs also exist; asset depletion typically wants 20%+ down. These let borrowers qualify without tax returns or W-2s.

**Jumbo:** Above the county conforming limit. Stricter — often 700+ (best near 740+), larger down payment, more reserves.

**Nashville market (2026):** Median sale price roughly $470K–$530K depending on source/submarket; condos ~$350–370K; downtown/luxury submarkets (Belle Meade, Green Hills, Forest Hills, Brentwood, Franklin) materially higher. Market has shifted toward balanced — more inventory, longer days on market (~60–98), modest 3–5% appreciation forecast. No-state-income-tax is a recurring in-migration driver.

**THDA (Tennessee Housing Development Agency):** Great Choice Loan (30-yr fixed + down payment assistance, homebuyer education required); Homeownership for the Brave (0.5% rate reduction for service members); down payment assistance commonly up to ~5% / capped (confirm current cap and rules before stating a dollar figure).

## User intent — answer before you sell

Before drafting each section, answer internally:

1. What's the single qualification question that drove this search?
2. What cost or rule are they afraid will disqualify them (low score, no W-2, high DTI, small down payment, property type)?
3. What's the number they actually want (rate, down payment, limit, monthly payment)?
4. What's the next step they're weighing right now (get a quote, get pre-approved, keep shopping lenders, give up)?

Resolve the qualification question, then the cost surprise, then the comparison, then the action — in that order. A reader who sees their exact scenario named keeps reading. A reader fed generic mortgage 101 bounces to the next tab.

## E-E-A-T and YMYL compliance

Mortgages are squarely YMYL: financial stability, major debt, long-term obligation. Treat the standards as a floor.

**Demonstrate:** real loan-structuring sophistication, current program knowledge, local Middle Tennessee market fluency, and honest cost comparison (FHA lifetime MIP vs. removable conventional PMI; DSCR rate premium vs. portfolio scalability; bank statement rate premium vs. approval where conventional declines).

**Avoid:** rate guarantees, "you'll definitely qualify" promises, hiding the rate premium on non-QM products, or implying assistance programs are free money without rules. Use cautious framing — "may," "typically," "depending on the lender/county," "as of [date], subject to change" — never "guaranteed," "always," "the lowest rate, period." ShopRates is a broker shopping many lenders; honest comparison is the brand.

## Article structure

### 1. Above-the-fold introduction

Open by naming the reader's exact situation and the specific product/number they came for — a self-employed buyer who can't show income on tax returns, an investor past the conventional cap, a veteran who didn't know $0 down still exists, a first-timer staring at a 580 score. Then preview the concrete payoff: the requirements, the real cost, and how ShopRates shops it across lenders.

Build momentum with specifics, not hype.

**Example — intro paragraph:**

*Weak:*
> Looking for a mortgage in Nashville? You've come to the right place. There are many loan options available, and we can help you find the best one. Read on to learn everything you need to know about getting a home loan in Tennessee.

*Strong:*
> If your tax returns show $40,000 after write-offs but your business actually clears $180,000, a conventional lender will decline you — and a bank statement loan will approve you on 12 months of deposits instead. This guide breaks down exactly how self-employed financing works in Tennessee: which program fits your deposit pattern, what credit score and down payment you'll need (typically 620+ and 10–25%), the rate premium to expect, and how shopping it across 60+ lenders changes the number you actually pay.

The strong version names the precise borrower, the disqualifier, the fix, the real requirements, and the broker value-add.

### 2. The loan program — depth required

For a product page (FHA, VA, USDA, conventional, DSCR, jumbo, bank statement, etc.), cover with specificity:

- **Who it's for** — the exact borrower profile, stated plainly
- **How qualification actually works** — score, down payment, DTI/ratio, income docs, property type
- **The real numbers** — current rate range, down payment %, limits, fees (upfront + ongoing)
- **The cost catch** — MIP vs PMI, funding fee, rate premium, annual fees — name it honestly
- **How it compares** — to the 1–2 alternatives this borrower is also considering
- **Tennessee/Nashville specifics** — county limits, eligible areas, THDA overlay where relevant

### 3. Eligibility and the numbers

Cover the qualification mechanics the reader is actually worried about:

- Credit score tiers and what each unlocks
- Down payment by scenario (and where assistance fills the gap)
- DTI / residual income / DSCR ratio thresholds
- Income documentation paths (W-2, tax returns, bank statements, P&L, 1099, assets)
- Property/occupancy rules (primary vs investment, eligible areas, unit count)
- Reserves and closing-cost expectations

### 4. Costs, rates, and the monthly payment

Cover: how the rate is built, points, APR vs note rate, PMI/MIP/funding/guarantee fees, closing costs in Tennessee, and a worked monthly-payment example at current rates. Be concrete and current; always tag rates as "as of [date], subject to change."

**Example — section opener tone:**

*Weak:*
> Closing costs are an important part of buying a home. There are many fees involved, so make sure you budget for them.

*Strong:*
> On a $470,000 Nashville purchase, plan for roughly 2–5% in closing costs — title, appraisal, origination, prepaids, and Tennessee-specific recording and transfer taxes. The line that surprises FHA buyers most is mortgage insurance: 1.75% upfront (rolled in) plus an annual premium for the life of the loan, unless you put 10% down. That lifetime MIP is exactly why a 700-score buyer with 5% down often pencils out cheaper on conventional with removable PMI — and why it's worth running both before you lock.

### 5. Local market / investor context (where relevant)

For market pages, city pages, and investor content, cover: current Nashville/TN median prices and trend, days-on-market and inventory, submarket differences, short-term-rental/Airbnb dynamics for investor pages, and the no-state-income-tax in-migration story. Keep it data-grounded, not boosterish.

These reduce pogo-sticking, answer the next question before it's asked, and signal depth to retrieval algorithms.

## Conversion architecture

ShopRates converts on **rate quotes and pre-approvals**, not panic calls. Place CTA moments at three points minimum:

1. **After the intro** — a soft "see your rate" prompt for the reader who already knows what they want
2. **Mid-article** — typically after the costs/rates section, when the reader is doing the math
3. **End** — a firmer next-step CTA tied to a specific action (get pre-approved, get a custom quote, talk to a broker)

CTA language matches reader state: confident, specific, no pressure. "See your personalized rate in minutes — no credit impact" outperforms "Contact us today." Lean on the broker advantage (shopping many lenders, soft-pull quotes) since that's the real differentiator.
